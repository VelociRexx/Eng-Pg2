Starship Troopers

Whether an adaptation is good or bad is irrelevant to this discussion, the only thing that matters is if the adaptation is faithful to the original material. Being faithful to the material is not always making the recreation a 1:1 of the original content, if that is even possible. Changing the material can still be successful, if you keep to the original themes and ideas, you can get away with changing the original, even in drastic ways. Starship Troopers is a good example of this, where the book is drastically different from the original. The book was a story that tackled concepts such as suffrage, corporal and capital punishment, and civil virtue. The movie tackles these concepts as well, but changes a memorable part of the original.


Starship Troopers the book is a novel published in 1959, in which we follow Juan "Johnny" Rico in his journey through his rise in the modern military’s “Mobile Infantry.” The book takes place roughly 700 years in the future, with the entire planet run by a world government. This world government came to be after the collapse of “western style democracies,” where anyone who holds full citizenship must earn it through Federal Service. Nobody is born with full citizenship, but basic rights, such as freedom of speech and assembly are given to all. Certain government positions are only available to full citizenship people. The book explores what a society run by veterans would look like, the morality of corporal/capital punishment , and what a society with equal individuals would look like with these ideas in place. A huge part of the book was that in the Mobile Infantry, or MI, each soldier was controlling a highly advanced power suit that looked almost like a gorilla. It was something similar to a Halo power suit. The novel ends with Johnny on his ship over the “bug” capital planet, about to drop to the planet with his platoon, of which his father had become a sergeant in.


Starship Troopers the movie, which came out in 1997 is very similar to the book, with a few plot changes to make it better in a movie format. These changes mostly include changing the ending to feel like a proper conclusion to a story, where a “thinking” bug, a leader for the otherwise mindless swarm is captured, giving humanity the edge, an edge they need to catapult their advantage in their war against the “bugs”. This is not the widely disputed part of the story, however. That part belongs to the movie’s change from a serious drama to a comedy, and instead of having power suits for each individual soldier, the standard MI infantry man was some light body armor, and a Morita MK1 (A rifle based on the Ruger Mini-14). The director changed the humans from highly advanced cybernetically enhanced warriors into a Russian human wave tactic. As one can imagine, hardcore fans of the book were mortified at the apparent mockery of their beloved novel, and attacked the movie. All you could hear about it was that it completely abandoned the ideas of the book, and replaced it with some campy humour and some not so indirect commentary on facism, such as the .


The question is, were those critics right? Was the movie a gross insult to the book's concepts and ideas, or did it absorb these ideas and put them in another light with a new lens? Plain and simple, Starship Troopers the movie is a great and faithful recreation of the original book. The movie brings forth discussion on most of the issues that the book was known for. The concept of full citizenship through service is very present in the movie, and the history of earth is largely the same as the novel. There are certain areas of the book that the movie even expands upon, such as the way a government run entirely by cold hearted veterans might lead to some moral dilemmas. A famous example of this in the book and movie would be the attack upon Buenos Aires, and humanity, by the bugs. The novel touches on the attack, but other than a driving force for Johnny when he learns his mother dies in the attack and the driving incident for the “bug” war, it is rarely mentioned again. In the movie, both of Johnny’s parents die, and there is countless propaganda shown throughout the movie made to be a rallying cry for war against the bugs. The big change they made to the movie was removing the power armour and making the troopers numerous and expendable, and turning the movie’s tone into more of a comedic one.


In short, while Starship Troopers is changed dramatically from its original novel, it still explores many of the same ideas that the novel did, and in some areas expanded what the book started. By keeping the main themes of the novel, Starship Troopers the movie is a faithful adaptation to the big screen
